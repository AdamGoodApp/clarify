It is extremely unlikely that you will be the only business with your product or service offering, so which businesses are your nearest competition?

It is important to consider that your competition might not necessarily offer exactly what you do, but might be something that attracts the same audience ‘spend’ (e.g. it could be argued that cinemas compete with video game producers, as they both compete for the same ‘entertainment’ spend).

2. What this is

What this is

You’ve just taken the first steps to realise and clarify your brand.

This might come as a surprise, as your brand might not be what you think it is.

Your brand is not your logo and colour scheme, or related to communications strategy or marketing plan – it is at the core of your business and should inform each of these elements.

In simple terms your brand is what explains and identifies who you are, what you do, how you do it – and the value of this to your clients, customers or users.

The next stages will help you realise and begin to clarify your brand simply.

Proposition example 1

A business based in the UK specialises in Chinese social media for UK brands. Their business and brand proposition is; ‘The only UK agency that puts your brand top-of-mind with the affluent Chinese individual through engaging, culturally appropriate social media content.’ It is important to note the emphasis and focus on the value seen by the client in this business’ proposition.

Proposition example 2

A management company who works across the media spectrum explains their business and brand proposition as follows; ‘The talent management company that develops strategic and creative ideas by marrying talent and companies in the media sector who are looking to generate new revenue streams through exciting, innovative projects for the public’s enjoyment.’ Again, note the highlighting of the value they deliver to their clients, both in helping create exciting, innovative projects and also in generating new revenue streams for the client.

You’ve explained why the business exists – let’s call this the purpose of the business.

What is at the core or the heart of the business? Importantly, also think about what the benefits of this are to customers or clients. If you want to develop a relationship with customers then it is becoming ever more important that your business has a purpose – other than making a profit – for them to relate to and share in. What you need to consider here is why should the purpose of your business be of interest to anyone else?

People’s experience of you will shape their opinion of you, and importantly how they then talk about you to their friends and wider social networks. Remember that people’s experience of your brand is not only shaped by what you say, so it is important to consider how people might talk about their experience of you. A London-based management consultancy thought beyond the obvious in their industry (hard working, trusted, driven), and reflected two points commonly raised internally and by their clients - their meritocratic nature, and that they're not afraid to make fun of themselves. Now Think

You’ve explained who your target audience are, let’s call this your market.

Are you putting limits on your potential audience that don’t actually exist, such as geographical location, etc? Is your market too broad, and therefore not allowing you to target people effectively, or too narrow, meaning you don’t get enough customers? Having a clear idea about your market is essential to developing a strategic approach that might appeal to them.

Be aware that your market is always developing and changing, so your market may be different in a year’s time.

Market example 1

A UK business-support company recognised that their audience are individuals seeking to turn their passion into a business. Or put another way, rather than thinking their market is anyone starting a business they identified the thousands of ‘kitchen-table start-ups’ through the UK. Clearly defining their target audience has allowed them to focus on this audience - both with the tone and type content they produce, their visual style, and in the new tools they're looking to develop in the future.

Market example 2

A business based in the UK specialises in Chinese social media for UK brands. Their market can be literally any UK business who want to better communicate with Chinese consumers through Chinese social media. This is a large market, so they have selected a few specific sectors to focus on. Sometimes it’s necessary to be selective and not try and throw your net too wide.

Now Think

Identifying your competition is an important first step.

In analysing your competition can you identify what they are doing that you aren’t, or can you see how they present themselves both in written content and visually? Are you clearly identifiable from your competition? You don’t want to be confused for someone else, or them to be confused for you. Your definition of who you are as a business should not be reliant on referencing your competition, but that doesn’t mean that you should ignore them. It is useful to know how you differ from your competitors, and in what areas you might have a competitive advantage.

As with your market, be aware that your competitors are always developing and changing themselves, and new competition is always being added.

Competition example 1

The competition for an online brand development tool - (ring any bells?) - extends beyond other online tools. Books, blog posts and videos, talks, brand agencies and brand consultants all compete for the same attention and revenue. Defining their competitors in a broader sense has allowed the creators to focus on points of difference - both in functionality and the way they communicate.

Competition example 2

A London-based management consultancy found that their competition included some very large businesses, or more specifically a certain section of very large businesses who specialise in a similar offering. Your competition may not simply be businesses who are of a similar size to you.
